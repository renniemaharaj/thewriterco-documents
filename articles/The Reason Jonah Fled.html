The Narrative

Alright, now that we have that out of the way, have you studied the book of Jonah before? I thought that I knew the story of Jonah until I took the time to study it recently. I realized that there's a much bigger picture than Jonah, the prophet, fleeing from the presence of God, tossed in the ocean, swallowed up by a great fish, repenting, and ultimately surrendering to God. By that narrative, the story appears to be about obedience, and while that narrative isn't far from the truth, it doesn't do justice either.

Jonah didn't flee due to his fear of Nineveh — or any fear, as you would think, for that matter. I mean, the guy fled from the presence of God — he slept through the storm, proposed that he should be tossed overboard, was swallowed by a great fish, and yet, dared to argue with the Most High God. Albeit, I wouldn't say that Jonah disrespected God. I think that their relationship was particular. A relationship that uniquely reveals more of God's personality and his interactions with man. To understand the true story of Jonah, we have to begin at chapter 1, verse 1.

The Book of Jonah

Jonah 1:1-2

(1:1) Now the word of the LORD came unto Jonah the son of Amittai, saying, (1:2) Arise, go to Nineveh, that great city, and cry against it; for their wickedness is come up before me.

Around 800 BC, 800 years before Christ Jesus, God spoke to the prophet Jonah, instructing him to go to the city of Nineveh and cry unto it. "For their wickedness is come up before me", God said. However, God didn't know that Jonah was on vacation. Jonah had other plans.

Jonah 1:3

But Jonah rose up to flee unto Tarshish from the presence of the LORD, and went down to Joppa; and he found a ship going to Tarshish: so he paid the fare thereof, and went down into it, to go with them unto Tarshish from the presence of the LORD.

Jonah fled from the presence of the LORD. He what? Jonah didn't know that the presence of the LORD was (is) the vocation—His spirit is. King David knew that.

They ultimately believed in Jonah's God and cast Jonah overboard. It's safe to say that Jonah had intended to drown and die (Judging by later remarks).

Then the word of God came to Jonah again, and this time he went. He cried to Nineveh, they repented, and God repented.

Jonah 3:1-5

(3:1) And the word of the LORD came unto Jonah the second time, saying, (3:2) Arise, go unto Nineveh, that great city, and preach unto it the preaching that I bid thee. (3:3) So Jonah arose, and went unto Nineveh, according to the word of the LORD. Now Nineveh was an exceeding great city of three days' journey. (3:4) And Jonah began to enter into the city a day's journey, and he cried, and said, Yet forty days, and Nineveh shall be overthrown. (3:5) So the people of Nineveh believed God, and proclaimed a fast, and put on sackcloth, from the greatest of them even to the least of them.

This displeased Jonah, and we see why Jonah fled.

Jonah 4:1-5

(4:1) But it displeased Jonah exceedingly, and he was very angry. (4:2) And he prayed unto the LORD, and said, I pray thee, O LORD, was not this my saying, when I was yet in my country? Therefore I fled before unto Tarshish: for I knew that thou art a gracious God, and merciful, slow to anger, and of great kindness, and repentest thee of the evil. (4:3) Therefore now, O LORD, take, I beseech thee, my life from me; for it is better for me to die than to live. (4:4) Then said the LORD, Doest thou well to be angry? (4:5) So Jonah went out of the city, and sat on the east side of the city, and there made him a booth, and sat under it in the shadow, till he might see what would become of the city.

So Jonah fled not out of fear for Nineveh, but because he knew that Nineveh would repent, and God would repent. Knowing that God is a gracious God, and merciful, slow to anger, and of great kindness, and repentest thee of the evil.

If anything, the story of Jonah is a testament to God's ridiculous goodness, so good that it even sometimes provokes his servants and prophets. God is good.
